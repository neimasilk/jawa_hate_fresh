## ABSTRACT

Hate-speech annotation in low-resource languages is bottlenecked by the cost and unreliability of human annotators. We present a fully-automated, zero-human annotation pipeline for Javanese — a language with roughly 80 million speakers but minimal computational resources — that filters an existing public Indonesian hate-speech dump for Javanese and code-mixed text and labels it by majority vote of three independent language models (two cloud, one free local), driven by a culturally-grounded four-dimensional taxonomy. From the dump we produce 728 labeled instances at a total cost of about six US dollars and zero human labor. Inter-model reliability of the released three-rater majority labels is moderate (Krippendorff alpha 0.51 held-out; the two-cloud-model pair reaches 0.69, raw agreement 0.89, Gwet AC1 0.82). Crucially, we run the external check the pipeline itself omitted: the labels agree with the dump's original human labels only 54.5% of the time (Cohen kappa 0.19), because the cultural prompt deliberately narrows hate to group-directed attacks and excludes bare profanity; on inspection the divergent cases are mostly defensible, and the texts the models newly flag are real Javanese and code-mixed identity slurs the Indonesian annotators missed. We argue this makes cross-model agreement necessary but not sufficient, and recommend a one-hour native-expert spot-check as a validity anchor that does not reintroduce the annotation bottleneck. We report methodological findings on prompt engineering, directional rater bias, and adversarial verification, and release the dataset, codebook, and pipeline.

**Keywords:** Javanese; hate speech detection; large language model; inter-annotator agreement; low-resource language

## 1. INTRODUCTION

Javanese is spoken by roughly 80 million people, yet it remains severely under-resourced in natural language processing [1], [2]. Hate speech on Indonesian social media frequently surfaces in Javanese or in Javanese–Indonesian code-mixing, carrying ethnic, religious, gender, and political animus that is specific to the Javanese cultural context: target groups such as Madurese, Chinese-Indonesian, and Arab communities; intra-Javanese regional rivalries; and a speech-level (*unggah-ungguh*) system in which the same hostile content can be delivered in coarse *ngoko* or in deceptively polite *krama* [3]. General-purpose or even general-Indonesian hate-speech taxonomies and datasets [4], [5], [6] do not capture these dimensions, and the small number of Javanese-specific efforts [7] remain unreleased.

The dominant methodology for building such resources assumes a team of trained native annotators reaching acceptable inter-annotator agreement. This assumption is unrealistic in a genuinely low-resource setting: a single part-time researcher with no budget for an annotation team. Worse, human annotation under these constraints can be actively harmful. This work is directly motivated by a failure: in a prior effort to build a Javanese hate-speech dataset, student annotators — working with limited supervision and throughput-oriented incentives — produced "Javanese" data largely by *back-translating* existing Indonesian and English hate speech rather than annotating authentic Javanese text. The corruption was structural and silent; the labels were internally consistent, so it surfaced only when downstream models failed to generalize, by which point the annotators had graduated and were unavailable. This is a concrete instance of why human-in-the-loop annotation, under realistic low-resource constraints, is *high-risk* rather than a gold standard.

Recent work shows that large language models can serve as competitive text annotators [8], [9], with reported inter-model agreement in ranges comparable to human teams [9]. However, this literature is overwhelmingly English and general-domain; whether *cultural, register-aware* prompting can make multi-model consensus reliable enough on authentic low-resource hate speech to replace human annotation entirely is open. We address this gap.

We make three primary contributions. First, a **fully-automated, zero-human annotation pipeline**: public dump → language-model Javanese filter → toxicity pre-screen → multi-model consensus labeling → automated reliability analysis, in which no human annotates or adjudicates any instance. Second, a **culturally-grounded four-dimensional taxonomy** (target group, severity, register, form), released as a formal codebook, that operationalizes a precise hate-vs-profanity boundary for Javanese; the *register* dimension (*ngoko/madya/krama*, including "polite-violent" *krama*) is, to our knowledge, novel as a taxonomic extension, though we show it is empirically near-dormant in social-media data and treat it as a proposed dimension to be tested on formal discourse rather than a demonstrated result. Third, a **validation methodology for human-free annotation** that does not flatter itself: cross-model Krippendorff alpha with bootstrap confidence intervals *and prevalence-robust coefficients*, a comparison against the source corpus's original human labels, vendor-sensitivity and directional-bias analysis, and adversarial verification of the analysis code. We further report transferable methodological findings (Section 4): principled prompt corrections raised agreement by Δα ≈ 0.23 with fixed models; the cloud raters carry a measurable directional bias; a cheap local pre-screen had only ~25% precision against cloud verification; and independent recomputation can replicate rather than catch analysis bugs.

We are explicit about what we do **not** claim. This is not a "first dataset" — Javanese hate-speech data has been built before [7], though not released; ours is the first *publicly released* culturally-grounded Javanese set. We do not claim that cross-model agreement establishes *correctness*: agreement measures shared reliability, not validity, and our raters share pretraining and may share bias. Most importantly, we do not claim the pipeline reproduces human judgment — Section 4.3 shows it agrees with the source human labels only 54.5% of the time — but rather that it applies a *different, narrower, and we argue more defensible* definition of hate, a claim we support qualitatively and recommend anchoring with a small expert spot-check. Modeling baselines are left as future work so that the contribution is the method, not an F1 number.

## 2. THE CULTURAL TAXONOMY (PROPOSED FRAMEWORK)

We define hate speech operationally as text that **attacks, demeans, dehumanizes, or incites violence or discrimination against a person or group based on group identity** — ethnicity, religion or belief, gender or sexual orientation, social class, or political collective. The decisive criterion is the *direction of attack toward group identity*, **not** the coarseness of the language. Javanese is rich in profanity (*pisuhan*: *asu*, *jancuk*, *cuk*) that functions as in-group bonding (*banter*) in the Arek/Surabaya register; coarseness alone is not hate. This distinction is the single most error-prone boundary and is encoded throughout the taxonomy [10].

The taxonomy has four dimensions (full definitions, decision rules, and worked examples appear in the released codebook):

**2.1. Target group** — ethnicity (*suku Madura, Tionghoa, Sunda, Batak, Arab*, …), intra-Javanese region (*Mataraman, Arek, Banyumasan*), religion or belief (*Islam, Kristen, Katolik, Hindu, Buddha, Konghucu, kepercayaan*), class (*kutha/ndeso, priyayi/cilik*), gender or LGBTQ, political collective (figure, party, mass organization), or *tidak ada* (none).

**2.2. Severity** — *BUK* (not hate) / *ringan* (light: stereotyping) / *sedang* (moderate: dehumanization, exclusion) / *berat* (severe: threats, incitement). The consistency rule is that any non-*BUK* level requires `hate:true`.

**2.3. Register (proposed; untested in this corpus)** — *ngoko* / *madya* / *krama* / *campur kasar*. Hate can in principle be carried in polite *krama* ("polite-violent"), which a register-aware taxonomy can capture where a register-blind one cannot. We include this dimension as a contribution to the *framework*, while reporting honestly (Section 4.5) that it is near-dormant in our social-media data (consensus *krama*-hate = 0); it is a hypothesis for formal-discourse sampling, not a demonstrated phenomenon here.

**2.4. Form** — *direct* / *sarcastic* / *idiomatic* (*pasemon*, cultural innuendo) / *code-switched*.

A key empirical finding (Section 4.4) is that, in social-media data, register is overwhelmingly *ngoko*, with pure *krama*-hate essentially absent. We report this honestly: the register dimension's value is its *capability* to capture polite-violent hate plus the empirical confirmation that social-media Javanese hate is *ngoko* with code-mixing — not that *krama*-hate is frequent.

## 3. METHOD

### 3.1. Data sourcing and language-model Javanese filtering

We avoid live scraping in favor of an existing public Indonesian hate-speech dump [4]. Because Javanese-specific corpora are scarce and a generic streaming source proved nearly hate-free — yielding a degenerate α = 1.0 with all-non-hate labels, a cautionary result in itself — we filter the Indonesian dump for Javanese and Javanese-code-mixed content using a language model as filter. The filter is precise, correctly separating Sundanese, Malay, and Portuguese as *other*; however, the density of "hot" Javanese (Javanese carrying potential toxicity) is low (~3–4%), so large pools require filtering many rows. Pure Javanese is near-zero on social media (1/250); *code-mixing is the empirical reality* [11], [12] and is accepted as in scope.

### 3.2. Cascade filter: cheap local pre-screen, cloud verification

To enlarge the pool affordably we use a cascade: a free local model pre-screens for likely-Javanese (8,349 → 3,088), a stronger free local model pre-screens for likely-toxic (→ 1,687), and a cloud model *verifies* the survivors so the final pool remains cloud-confirmed. The cloud model confirmed only **25.4%** of the local pre-screen's keeps (1,687 → +304), i.e. the cheap local pre-screen substantially over-keeps. The cascade still saves cloud calls on the rejected majority, but cloud verification remains the precision and cost bottleneck — a concrete, quantified trade-off for practitioners. The single dump was exhausted at 12.7K filtered rows, ceiling-ing the pool at 735; scaling the dataset requires *new sources*, not deeper filtering.

### 3.3. Cultural prompt engineering

We iterate the labeling prompt on a fixed 149-text pool, measuring the agreement of the primary rater pair after each version (Table 1).

**Table 1.** Prompt iteration and inter-rater agreement (primary pair, n = 149).

| Prompt | Change | α (ds+grok) | 95% CI |
|---|---|---|---|
| v0 | initial cultural taxonomy + 5 few-shot examples | 0.534 | — |
| v1 | hate := group-directed; profanity ≠ hate; fixed a contradictory example | 0.554 | — |
| v2 | + "identity slur aimed at an individual is hate"; +2 few-shot examples | **0.763** | [0.624, 0.879] |

(All three cells are the same two-rater set, DeepSeek+Grok, on the same 149-text pool; an earlier draft mistakenly listed a 3-rater value for v0.) Two principled definition corrections raised α by **Δ ≈ +0.23 without changing the models**. A methodological caution surfaced here: α can stay flat (v0→v1) even when labels qualitatively improve, because skewed prevalence inflates chance agreement; one must read the flip table and raw agreement alongside α. We stopped at v2: the residual disagreements were genuinely ambiguous (meta-commentary, quoted hate, positive comparisons) and chasing them on the evaluation pool would risk overfitting. They are documented in the codebook as boundary cases.

### 3.4. Multi-model consensus labeling

Final labels are the majority vote of three independent raters: two cloud models (DeepSeek [16] and Grok) and one local, free model (Qwen3-14B [17]). The free local rater strengthens triangulation and makes the pipeline partially reproducible without paid APIs. Vendor selection was itself empirical (Section 4.5): one slow, low-validity reasoning model was dropped, and a *region-native* model failed as a rater despite its specialization, while the general local model passed. Per-vendor operating characteristics appear in Table 2.

**Table 2.** Per-vendor characteristics on the full pool (n = 735).

| Vendor | Refusal % | JSON valid % | Latency (ms) | Cost (USD) | Hate rate |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| DeepSeek | 0.7 | 96.5 | 15,409 | 3.63 | 22% |
| Grok | 0.1 | 99.9 | 5,179 | 2.49 | 28% |
| Qwen3-14B (local) | 0.1 | 99.7 | 10,869 | 0.00 | 14% |

Total cost, including merged prompt-iteration calls, was **USD 6.13**. Explicit refusals are negligible (≤ 0.7%); effective per-rater attrition (refusal plus empty/invalid responses) is up to ~3.7% (DeepSeek returned no usable label on 27/735), so the usable-label rate is ~96%. With this caveat, safety alignment is not a blocker for hate-speech classification *when framed explicitly as forensic academic analysis* — a framing dependency we do not over-generalize.

### 3.5. Validation design

Validation has four layers. (i) **Cross-model reliability** — Krippendorff α for nominal data in its canonical coincidence-matrix form, with 5,000-sample bootstrap confidence intervals, treating the models as raters [13]; because hate prevalence is skewed (~22%), we report raw agreement and the prevalence-robust Gwet AC1 [23] alongside α. (ii) **Held-out split** — α computed separately on the texts *outside* the prompt-iteration pool; we report the held-out-minus-tuning α difference and its CI rather than asserting equivalence from overlapping intervals. (iii) **External comparison** — because the source dump carries original human labels, we report the confusion matrix and agreement between our consensus labels and those human labels (Section 4.3) — the one external signal available, which the pipeline must not ignore. (iv) **Adversarial verification** — the analysis code is audited by independent agents *and* recomputed from scratch. We emphasize that (i)–(iv) bound *reliability* and *self-consistency*, not *validity*; cross-model agreement cannot certify correctness when raters share pretraining. We therefore recommend, but have not yet performed, a ~100-item native-expert spot-check stratified on the human-label disagreements (the project's fallback-ladder option, ~1 hour, categorically lighter than full annotation) as the validity anchor.

## 4. RESULTS AND DISCUSSION

### 4.1. Dataset characterization

The pipeline yields **728 consensus instances** (158 hate, 21.7%; 570 non-hate), of which **569 are unanimous** and only **7 are ties**. After text normalization 706 of the 735 labeled texts are unique (149 of 158 hate are distinct), so we report effective unique counts where they matter. Target-group counts are computed by a single stated rule — a category is assigned to a hate instance if it appears in ≥2 of that instance's valid votes (the same majority principle as the binary label), regenerated by `audit.py` for reproducibility. By that rule the leading targets among hate instances are *gender_wanita* (46), *politik_partai* (19), *gender_lgbtq* (18), *suku_tionghoa* (14), *agama_islam* (8), *politik_tokoh* (8), *politik_ormas* (7), and *agama_kristen* (7); Arab, belief, and other categories have ≤3 and are not analysed per-category. (Earlier drafts reported higher figures computed by summing per-vendor votes rather than per-record majorities; the majority-rule figures here supersede them.) Severity is dominated by *sedang* and *ringan*; *berat* (incitement, threats) is rare. Register is 157/158 *ngoko* (Section 4.5); form is overwhelmingly *direct*.

### 4.2. Inter-model reliability

The released labels are a **three-rater majority vote**, so the reliability that actually governs the dataset is the three-rater α: **0.513** held-out (n=586) and **0.545** on the full pool — "moderate," and on the held-out split below the 0.6 threshold we adopt. We state this plainly rather than headlining a more flattering subset. The two cloud models alone (DeepSeek+Grok) agree considerably more (α 0.688 held-out, 0.701 full), but they are only part of the labeling rule, so we report the cloud pair as an upper sensitivity bound, not as the reliability of the shipped labels. Because hate prevalence is skewed (~22%), α penalizes the minority class; raw agreement and Gwet AC1 are substantially higher and we report them too (Table 3).

**Table 3.** Reliability per rater set (binary hate). α = Krippendorff (canonical), raw = observed agreement, AC1 = Gwet's prevalence-robust coefficient.

| Rater set | subset | n | α | 95% CI | raw | AC1 |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| DeepSeek+Grok | held-out | 561 | 0.688 | [0.617, 0.757] | 0.886 | 0.820 |
| DeepSeek+Grok | full | 707 | 0.701 | [0.636, 0.760] | 0.890 | 0.825 |
| DeepSeek+qwen3 | held-out | 560 | 0.401 | [0.301, 0.497] | 0.830 | 0.763 |
| Grok+qwen3 | held-out | 583 | 0.398 | [0.308, 0.488] | 0.806 | 0.714 |
| **3-rater (released labels)** | held-out | 586 | **0.513** | [0.451, 0.572] | — | — |
| **3-rater (released labels)** | full | 735 | **0.545** | [0.490, 0.598] | — | — |

**On overfitting.** The cloud-pair α is 0.747 on the tuning pool (n=146) versus 0.688 held-out. Bootstrapping the difference (tuning − held-out, 8,000 resamples) gives **+0.058, 95% CI [−0.09, +0.20]**. The interval includes zero, but it is also wide: with these sample sizes the minimum detectable difference at 80% power is ≈0.21, so the study has only ~27% power to detect a realistic 0.1 overfit. We therefore do **not** claim the prompt "generalizes" in an equivalence sense — overlapping CIs are not an equivalence test. We claim only the weaker, supportable statement: held-out cloud-pair reliability clears 0.6 and is not *detectably* worse than the tuning pool at this sample size. qwen3 is the noisiest rater (Section 4.4) and drags the three-rater α down; we retain it as a free, reproducible third vote rather than excluding it to inflate the headline.

### 4.3. Comparison with the source human labels

The source dump is itself human-labeled (a binary `hate`/`neutral` field). The pipeline does not use these labels, but they are the only external signal available, so we report the comparison rather than hide it (Table 4). Over all 728 consensus instances, our labels agree with the human labels only **54.5%** of the time (Cohen κ = **0.19**), and our consensus calls non-hate on **307 of 441 (70%)** of the texts the human annotators flagged as hate.

**Table 4.** Consensus label vs. source human label (n = 728).

| | human = hate | human = neutral |
|---|---|---|
| **consensus = hate** | 134 | 24 |
| **consensus = non-hate** | 307 | 263 |

Taken naively, κ = 0.19 looks fatal for a "replace humans" pipeline. But the disagreement is overwhelmingly **definitional, not erroneous**, and in a direction the paper argues for: the source dataset conflates *abusive language* with *hate speech*, whereas our taxonomy (Section 2) deliberately excludes bare profanity and personal insults that lack a group-identity dimension. Manually inspecting the 307 "dropped" cases, the large majority are coarse but non-group-directed insults (e.g. profanity aimed at an individual) that our codebook scores `BUK` by design. Conversely, the **24** texts our models flag that the human annotators marked neutral are, on inspection, mostly genuine Javanese and code-mixed identity slurs the Indonesian-context annotators missed — e.g. ethnic (*sipit*, *aseng*), gender/sexuality (*banci*, *jablay*), and religious (*kafir*) slurs. This is concrete evidence that the cultural prompt catches real slurs a generic Indonesian pipeline misses. We are nonetheless honest about the residue: a small audit of dropped cases containing an identity-slur token (11 records) found at least one true coverage gap — a skin-colour/physical insult (*"ireng jomok"*) excused only because the v2 taxonomy lacks a physical-trait target category (a fix flagged in Section 4.9). The bottom line: cross-model agreement plus this definitional comparison cannot establish *validity*; that is exactly why we recommend the targeted expert spot-check (Section 3.5).

### 4.4. Vendor sensitivity and directional rater bias

The three raters are not exchangeable. Per-model hate rates differ systematically — Grok 27.9%, DeepSeek 21.9%, qwen3 14.2% — and the DeepSeek–Grok disagreement is asymmetric: Grok labels hate-without-DeepSeek 56 times versus DeepSeek-without-Grok 22 times (McNemar exact p = 1.5×10⁻⁴). Grok has a lower hate threshold; qwen3 a higher one. Consequently the majority vote is partly *arbitrating a fixed directional offset* rather than averaging independent noise, and the low-prevalence qwen3 tiebreaker pulls contested labels toward non-hate. qwen3 is also the noisiest rater overall (pairwise α 0.40 vs the cloud pair's 0.69; Table 3), which is why the three-rater α (0.51) sits below the cloud pair. We keep qwen3 for cost and reproducibility but no longer describe the raters as independent or exchangeable.

### 4.5. The register dimension is dormant in this corpus

Theory predicts polite-violent *krama* as a distinctive Javanese hate carrier. Empirically it does not appear: by majority vote, register is **157/158 *ngoko*** among hate instances and consensus *krama*-hate is **zero**. We report this as a negative result. The register dimension's contribution is therefore to the *framework* (it can represent polite-violent hate, and it confirms that social-media Javanese hate is *ngoko* with code-mixing), not a demonstrated phenomenon in this data. Testing it requires sampling formal or elite discourse — comment sections are the wrong venue — which we leave to future work.

### 4.6. Cascade economics

The local pre-screen's 25.4% confirmation rate (Section 3.2) shows that free local models can cheaply *reduce* a candidate pool but cannot replace cloud verification for precision; the economically rational design is local-recall-then-cloud-precision, with cloud cost scaling with pre-screen over-keep.

### 4.7. Region-native is not necessarily a better rater

In a single comparison, a regionally specialized Southeast-Asian model was a worse consensus rater (α 0.422, noise in both directions) than a general 14-billion-parameter model (α 0.660). We flag this only as a **single observation, not a general law**: each α is measured against one anchor rater on ~140–149 texts, the confidence intervals overlap ([0.238, 0.581] vs [0.480, 0.807]), and model size (8B vs 14B) is confounded with regional specialization. It suggests — but does not establish — that general instruction-following capacity can outweigh language-specific pretraining for structured classification; the region-native model remained useful for the easier filtering task.

### 4.8. Adversarial verification

Before release, the analysis was audited by independent agents and independently recomputed; a later, harder audit (which produced several of the corrections in this revision) repeated the exercise. The first round caught two real bugs: a deduplication load-order bug in which stale records overwrote valid labels (which had inflated an earlier α to an artefactual 0.763), and a non-canonical α formula (pooled-pairs rather than the weighted coincidence matrix), both fixed and re-validated against a reference dataset with known α = 0.743. One instructive episode occurred: an independent recomputation that *shared the same code-level assumptions* replicated the dedup bug and "confirmed" the wrong number, so only the code audit caught it. We do not over-generalize from one episode — a genuinely from-scratch recomputation reproduces the corrected canonical numbers exactly — but the practical lesson stands: verification should include at least one path that does not inherit the original's assumptions.

### 4.9. Limitations

We are candid about the threats to this work. (1) **Validity vs. reliability.** All automated checks bound *reliability* and *self-consistency*; none establish *validity*, and our raters share pretraining, so correlated error is possible and invisible to agreement. Section 4.3 shows the labels diverge sharply (κ 0.19) from the only human signal available. This is the central limitation; we recommend a ~100-item expert spot-check (Section 3.5) before strong validity claims. (2) **Source bias.** A single dump skews targets toward gender, politics, Chinese, and Islam and under-represents Madurese, Sundanese, Batak, and the *krama* register; nine of the populated target categories have ≤2 instances, so per-category analysis is not possible for them. (3) **Size and duplicates.** Of 735 texts, 706 are unique after normalization (149 of 158 hate are distinct); five held-out texts duplicate tuning-pool texts, a small (~1%) leakage we remove before the held-out computation in the released analysis. The effective unique-hate N (≈149) should be used wherever the 158 figure carries weight. (4) **Taxonomy coverage gap.** The audit found a physical-trait/skin-colour insult (*"ireng jomok"*) with no matching target category; v1.1 of the taxonomy should add a physical-trait target. (5) **Severity** is the noisiest dimension (raters agree on 89/158 hate instances; 69/158 lack a strict-unanimous severity), so the core released label is binary and severity carries a low-reliability flag. (6) The hardest boundary is *use vs. mention* — quoting/criticizing a slur vs. deploying it — which current model raters do not reliably distinguish.

### 4.10. Ethics

All texts are from public posts. Handles and mentions were removed and URLs placeholdered by the pipeline; the audit additionally found two residual phone numbers (e.g. a WhatsApp contact in a solicitation post), which a dedicated contact-information scrub removes before release (`experiments/audit_external/clean_release.py`) — we note this to be transparent that anonymization required a second pass. No re-identification is attempted and no private messages or groups are used. The released dataset is the cleaned version; the dataset is licensed CC BY-NC-SA 4.0 for research and moderation use only, **contingent on confirming that the source dump's licence permits a derived release** (flagged for the literature/legal pass). The models perform classification, not generation of offensive content, and explicit refusals were negligible (Section 3.4).

## 5. CONCLUSION

We presented a fully-automated, culturally-grounded pipeline that produces a Javanese hate-speech dataset with zero human annotation at a total cost of USD 6.13. The released three-rater majority labels have moderate inter-model reliability (α 0.51 held-out; 0.69 for the two-cloud-model pair, raw agreement 0.89). We deliberately do not claim the pipeline reproduces human labels — it agrees with the source human annotations only 54.5% of the time — but rather that it applies a narrower, group-directed definition of hate that, on inspection, both excludes bare profanity the source over-flagged and catches Javanese and code-mixed identity slurs the source missed. Our methodological contribution is a validation regime honest about its own limits (prevalence-robust reliability, an external comparison to human labels, directional-bias analysis, and adversarial code verification) together with transferable findings on prompt engineering, rater bias, and cascade economics. The single highest-priority next step is a small native-expert spot-check stratified on the human-label disagreements, which would convert the κ-0.19 divergence from a liability into evidence that the LLMs are right where they diverge — without reintroducing the annotation bottleneck. Further work will add sources to populate under-represented target groups, add a physical-trait target category, test the polite-violent *krama* hypothesis on formal discourse, and train baseline models as dataset characterization.
